Flood Protection

Warning Signs

  • Heavy rainfall forecast for multiple days
  • River water levels rising beyond normal
  • Waterlogging in low-lying areas of fields
  • Slow drainage of water from fields

Pre-Flood Preparation

Completed (2/5)

  • Clear drainage channels around fields
  • Identify high ground for equipment storage
  • Harvest mature crops if possible
  • Stock sandbags for emergency barriers
  • Move livestock to higher ground

During Flood

  1. Assess crop damage - some crops may recover if flood duration was short
  2. Clean debris from fields
  3. Test soil for contamination before replanting
  4. Apply gypsum to help remove salt deposits
  5. Reseed or transplant as soon as possible

Insurance Claim Process

Document damage with photos/videos showing extent and date

Notify insurance company within 72 hours of damage

Complete claim form with details of crops affected

Compensation typically paid within 30 days of claim approval

Crop-Specific Advice

Rice

Rice can survive 3-4 days of complete submersion if water is moving. Drain fields as soon as possible.

Wheat

Wheat is very sensitive to waterlogging. Even 2-3 days can cause significant yield loss.

Cotton

Cotton plants may survive if water recedes within 48 hours. Watch for root rot diseases afterward.

Vegetables

Most vegetables cannot survive flooding. Replant short-duration varieties after water recedes.

Drought Management

Warning Signs

  • Below-normal or no rainfall for several weeks
  • Rapid decline in soil moisture and surface water
  • Drooping or rolling leaves, delayed flowering
  • Lower water table in wells and boreholes
  • Reduced growth and patchy crop emergence

When Drought Strikes — Immediate Actions

Emergency Response

  • Prioritize irrigation for young plants and critical growth stages (flowering, grain filling)
  • Use deficit or alternate furrow irrigation to stretch water supplies
  • Apply mulch to conserve soil moisture and reduce evaporation
  • Avoid deep tillage that exposes soil moisture; reduce field operations
  • Move livestock to areas with shade and secure water; ration fodder carefully
  • Postpone nitrogen fertiliser applications — delay until moisture improves
  • Document crop stress and losses (photos, dates) for insurance and relief
  • Contact local extension/KVK for targeted advice and emergency support

Water Conservation Practices

  • Install or maintain drip and micro-sprinkler systems for efficient use
  • Adopt alternate furrow irrigation (water every other furrow) where appropriate
  • Collect and use rainwater or farm ponds for supplemental irrigation
  • Use soil conditioners (organic matter) to improve water holding capacity
  • Practice conservation tillage and residue retention

Pre-Drought Preparation

  • Prepare on-farm water storage (ponds, tanks) and maintain pumps
  • Choose drought-tolerant or short-duration varieties for critical plots
  • Plan crop rotations and stagger planting dates to reduce simultaneous water demand
  • Maintain irrigation infrastructure and check for leaks
  • Train labour and family on water-saving irrigation scheduling

Post-Drought Recovery

  • Assess plant survival and replant short-duration crops where feasible
  • Apply organic amendments to restore soil structure and moisture retention
  • Monitor for pest/disease outbreaks following drought stress
  • Apply for relief/insurance with documented evidence of losses

Crop-Specific Advice

Cereals (Wheat, Rice, Maize)

Provide supplemental irrigation during flowering and grain fill, consider early sowing of tolerant varieties. For rice, consider alternate wetting and drying when water-limited.

Pulses

Pulses are relatively drought-tolerant; focus on timely sowing and conserving moisture during germination.

Fruits and Trees

Deep, infrequent watering is better than shallow frequent watering; protect roots with mulch and reduce fruit load where necessary.

Government Support & Schemes

Check schemes such as PMFBY (Pradhan Mantri Fasal Bima Yojana) for crop insurance and state-level relief programmes. Local agriculture offices and KVKs can advise on available support.

Pest Outbreaks

Warning Signs

  • Sudden appearance of insects on leaves, stems, or fruits
  • Holes in leaves or chewed edges
  • Sticky honeydew on leaves (aphid infestation)
  • Wilting or yellowing of plants despite adequate water
  • Presence of ants farming aphids or mealybugs
  • Webbing on plants (spider mites)

Immediate Actions

Emergency Response

  • Identify the pest species correctly
  • Remove heavily infested plant parts
  • Apply appropriate organic/chemical treatment
  • Monitor neighboring plants for spread
  • Document damage for insurance claims

Prevention Measures

  • Practice crop rotation to break pest cycles
  • Use resistant varieties when available
  • Maintain proper plant spacing for good air circulation
  • Remove weeds that harbor pests
  • Encourage beneficial insects (ladybugs, lacewings)
  • Use pheromone traps for early detection

Treatment Options

Organic Methods

Neem oil spray (2ml/L water), soap solution, garlic-chili spray, beneficial nematodes

Chemical Control

Use only registered pesticides, follow label instructions, maintain safety intervals

Common Pests by Crop

Rice

Brown planthopper, stem borer, leaf folder - Monitor water levels and avoid excess nitrogen

Cotton

Bollworm, whitefly, aphids - Use BT cotton varieties and proper irrigation

Vegetables

Aphids, caterpillars, mites - Regular monitoring and early intervention crucial

Disease Control

Warning Signs

  • Spots, lesions, or discoloration on leaves
  • Wilting despite adequate moisture
  • Stunted growth or abnormal plant development
  • Powdery or fuzzy growth on plant surfaces
  • Root rot with foul smell
  • Sudden death of plant parts or entire plants

Immediate Actions

Disease Management

  • Identify disease symptoms accurately
  • Remove and destroy infected plant material
  • Apply appropriate fungicide treatment
  • Improve air circulation and reduce humidity
  • Sterilize tools and equipment

Prevention Strategies

  • Use disease-resistant varieties
  • Practice proper crop rotation
  • Maintain optimal plant spacing
  • Avoid overhead watering
  • Ensure good soil drainage
  • Regular field sanitation

Common Diseases

Fungal Diseases

Powdery mildew, rust, blight, root rot - Use copper-based fungicides

Bacterial Diseases

Bacterial wilt, leaf spot - Remove infected plants, use copper sprays

Viral Diseases

Mosaic, yellowing - Control vectors, use virus-free planting material

Crop-Specific Diseases

Rice

Blast, bacterial blight, sheath blight - Monitor weather conditions

Wheat

Rust, powdery mildew, head blight - Use resistant varieties

Tomato

Early blight, late blight, bacterial wilt - Proper staking and pruning

Treatment Guidelines

Apply fungicides in early morning
Follow safety intervals before harvest

Hailstorm Protection

Warning Signs

  • Dark, towering cumulonimbus clouds
  • Sudden drop in temperature
  • Strong winds preceding the storm
  • Weather alerts from IMD
  • Lightning and thunder activity
  • Greenish tint in clouds (hail indicator)

Protective Measures

Pre-Storm Preparation

  • Install hail nets or protective covers
  • Move portable equipment to shelter
  • Harvest mature crops if possible
  • Secure loose materials and structures
  • Monitor weather updates continuously

During Hailstorm

  • Seek immediate shelter - avoid working in fields
  • Stay away from metal structures and equipment
  • Monitor crop damage from safe location
  • Document damage with photos/videos
  • Wait for storm to completely pass before inspection

Post-Hailstorm Recovery

  • Assess crop damage immediately after storm
  • Remove severely damaged plant parts
  • Apply fungicide to prevent secondary infections
  • Provide extra nutrition to help recovery
  • Consider replanting if damage is extensive
  • File insurance claims within 72 hours

Protection Methods

Hail Nets

Install overhead nets with 20-30mm mesh size, maintain 2-3m height above crops

Greenhouse/Polytunnel

Most effective protection for high-value crops, use UV-stabilized covers

Crop Insurance

Essential for financial protection, covers yield loss due to hail damage

Crop Vulnerability

High Risk

Tomatoes, grapes, apples, mangoes - Require immediate protection

Medium Risk

Cotton, sugarcane, maize - Monitor weather alerts closely

Lower Risk

Rice, wheat, groundnut - Less susceptible to hail damage

Heat Wave Protection

Warning Signs

  • Temperature above 40°C for 3+ consecutive days
  • Low humidity levels (below 30%)
  • Strong, hot winds
  • Weather department heat wave alerts
  • Plant wilting despite adequate soil moisture
  • Sunburn on fruits and leaves

Protective Measures

Heat Management — Practical Measures

  • Irrigation: use short, more frequent waterings (cycles) to keep the root zone cool — prioritise flowering and grain-filling stages.
  • Mulch: apply organic (straw, crop residue) or plastic mulch to reduce evaporation and soil temperature; maintain 3–5 cm cover around plants.
  • Shade: install shade nets (30–50% for vegetables; 50% for nursery beds) or temporary covers during peak heat hours.
  • Anti-transpirants & Kaolin: consider kaolin clay sprays (1–3% suspension) or approved antitranspirant products to reduce sunburn — test on a small area first and follow product label.
  • Evaporative cooling / Misting: where water availability allows, short-duration misting in the hottest hours can cool canopy temperature for high-value crops; avoid overuse to conserve water.
  • Reflective mulches: use reflective films in orchards/specialty crops to lower soil temperature and reduce fruit sunburn.
  • Windbreaks: plant or use temporary windbreaks to reduce hot, desiccating winds which increase plant water loss.
  • Nutrition: avoid heavy nitrogen top-dressings during extreme heat; apply potassium/calcium-based foliar feeds to improve stress tolerance if recommended by extension.
  • Livestock: provide shaded, ventilated housing, cool drinking water, and reduce grazing during peak heat; consider sprinklers for large animals where feasible.
  • Worker safety: schedule labour early morning/late evening, provide potable water, shaded rest areas, and first-aid for heat exhaustion.
  • Monitor & document: use thermometers/moisture sensors, record crop symptoms and photos for insurance/extension support.

Note: Always follow product labels and local extension advice before applying sprays; test new treatments on a small area first.

When Heat Wave Strikes — Immediate Actions

Immediate Steps

  • Water early morning and late evening; prioritise flowering and fruiting stages
  • Apply mulch immediately to reduce evaporation
  • Install temporary shade nets or covers for vulnerable plots
  • Move livestock to shaded, ventilated areas and provide cool drinking water
  • Avoid heavy field work during peak hours to reduce worker heat risk
  • Delay or reduce nitrogenous fertiliser applications until conditions improve
  • Consider foliar kaolin spray for sunburn protection where appropriate
  • Document damage (photos, dates) and notify extension services if severe

Irrigation Management

  • Water early morning (5-8 AM) or late evening (6-8 PM)
  • Increase irrigation frequency but reduce duration
  • Use drip irrigation for efficient water delivery
  • Monitor soil moisture with moisture meters
  • Avoid overhead irrigation during peak heat

Shade and Protection

  • Install shade nets (30-50% shade)
  • Use temporary covers for sensitive crops
  • Plant windbreaks to reduce hot wind impact
  • Apply kaolin clay spray for sunburn protection
  • Use reflective mulches to reduce soil temperature

Crop-Specific Measures

Vegetables

Provide shade, increase watering, harvest early morning, use heat-tolerant varieties

Fruits

Protect from sunburn, maintain soil moisture, avoid pruning during heat stress

Cereals

Monitor grain filling stage, provide adequate irrigation, avoid late planting
